Introduction

“Aerodynamics are for people who can’t build engines” – Enzo Ferrari

Aerodynamics plays a vital role in F1 cars today. Without it, cars would not be able to corner at high speeds, accelerate without taking off the ground and provide competitive racing. The FIA controls regulations in F1 and encourage the use of aerodynamics and hybrid technology as it allows for closer racing and overtaking, providing a more entertaining race. Furthermore, the use of aerodynamics combined with new electrical systems in F1 cars allow for higher speeds on straights and more strategic ways of racing including DRS (Drag Reduction System) and ERS (Energy Recovery System) which are essential for overtaking in races. However the use of the hybrid engines is extremely expensive and a shift from the large V10 engines used in previous years, which viewers enjoyed.

The pre-aerodynamics era

The first Formula One race took place on May 13th, 1950, at the Silverstone Circuit in the United Kingdom. Competitors such as Ferrari, Alfa Romeo and Maserati took part where over 100,000 people watched the grand prix. The cars that took part had a low power-to-weight ratio, simple engineering, heavy steering and zero use of aerodynamics. The lack of aerodynamics led to limited downforce and poor cornering speed focusing on straight lines to gain speed (the opposite of what Formula One is known for today).

Aerodynamics introduced: Wings & Ground Effect

In the late 1960s, Team Lotus introduced innovative designs that incorporated aerodynamics by adding wings to the car by inverting the aerofoil of an airplane wing on the Lotus 49B. While wings on an aeroplane generate lift to fly, the aerofoil does the opposite and generates downforce to grip the road. This was the beginning of the scientific approach to Formula One which is still used to this day. The man behind the development was Colin Chapman, a design engineer and inventor who discovered ground effect aerodynamics. The first use of venturi tunnels was on the Lotus 78 in 1977.

While the 78 achieved 7 grand prix wins and 9 pole positions, the car experienced a severe number of problems with the chassis, as the stress generated by the downforce caused the suspension to become unstable. The following season was won by Team Lotus and driver Mario Andretti in the Lotus 79.

The modern era: turbo-hybrid

The current era of F1 cars is extremely dependent on aerodynamics and even use smaller turbo-charged engines as well as new hybrid systems that use electric motor-generators that provide even more power to the wheels. The DRS, also known as Drag Reduction System, lets the driver open a flap on the rear wing to increase straight-line speed and reduce drag. This new technology allows for closer racing.

DRS and ERS systems

DRS (Drag Reduction System)

The DRS was first introduced in 2011 to encourage and increase overtaking opportunities. Overtaking before DRS was much harder due to turbulent or "dirty" air produced by cars in front. The DRS prevents turbulent air by opening the flap on the rear wing when activated in specific zones. Opening the flap reduces drag and downforce and can increase top speed by approximately 6–7.5 mph. Activation rules ensure it can only be used in designated zones and when a following car is within one second.

ERS (Energy Recovery System)

ERS was introduced in 2014 (evolving from KERS first used in 2009). ERS combines two units: the MGU‑K (Motor Generator Unit — Kinetic) and the MGU‑H (Motor Generator Unit — Heat). MGU‑K recovers kinetic energy from braking and can return up to ~160 hp to the drivetrain. MGU‑H recovers heat energy from the turbocharger exhaust gases and can convert it to electricity to charge the battery or directly feed the MGU‑K, helping eliminate turbo lag.

Discussion

The DRS has a mixed reception: some viewers believe it simplifies overtaking and reduces purity, while others appreciate the increased wheel-to-wheel action. ERS is generally well received because it adds depth and strategy — drivers decide when to deploy or 'derate' to recharge the battery. When DRS and ERS are used together they provide combined opportunities for overtaking that change race dynamics significantly.

Conclusion

Overall, DRS and ERS have changed F1 significantly by adding strategic depth and new systems that affect racecraft, overtaking and energy management. Despite controversy, the balance of entertainment and technical progression suggests these technologies will continue to shape F1.
